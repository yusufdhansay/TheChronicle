Central banks have basically decided to chill out

After two pretty hectic years, the people in charge of interest rates are taking a breath. Here’s what that actually means for you.

By Eleanor Haight Economics Editor
November 14, 2024

It has been an exhausting cycle for global markets. From the sudden shocks of 2022 to the persistent inflationary pressures of 2023, the narrative has been one of constant reaction. However, as we approach the final quarter, a new sentiment is emerging from the marble halls of the world’s most powerful financial institutions: stillness.

Jerome Powell’s recent rhetoric suggests a significant pivot—not necessarily towards aggressive cuts, but towards a period of observation. The "higher for longer" mantra is being replaced by a more nuanced "wait and see." For the first time in twenty-four months, the pressure to act decisively has been supplanted by the luxury of time.

"The era of panic-driven hikes has concluded. We are now entering the era of the Great Plateaus."

What does this "chill" mean for the average investor? Primarily, it signifies a return to fundamentals. In a world where central bank policy isn't the only headline-grabbing variable, corporate earnings and true economic productivity can once again take center stage. The noise is subsiding, allowing the signal to finally emerge.

Across the Atlantic, the ECB is mirroring this contemplative stance. While the challenges in the Eurozone differ in their specifics—energy transitions and geopolitical tensions remain high—the collective sigh of relief from the governing council is audible. They have reached the summit of their tightening mountain and are now simply surveying the landscape.

However, "chilling out" is not synonymous with complacency. The risk of policy error remains the shadow that follows every meeting. If the labor market cools too rapidly or if energy prices see another shock, the central banks may find their newfound peace short-lived. For now, though, the world watches and waits, enjoying a rare moment of central bank predictability.
